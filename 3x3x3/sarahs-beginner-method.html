How to Solve a Rubik's Cube

Printable Version

This is a tutorial on how to solve a Rubik's Cube for those who have little or no experience with cubing.

Unlike most Rubik's Cube tutorials, this one is driven by current research in the field of education. The author is both an educator and a speedcuber.

About This Tutorial

If you want to learn how to solve the cube, you've come to the right place!

  • This tutorial is friendly for kids, teens, and adults.
  • This tutorial is friendly for left-handed, right-handed, and ambidextrous people.
  • This tutorial provides choices along the way.
  • This tutorial focuses on learning and understanding over memorizing.

The Cross

The cross is just another name for this plus sign shape.

When we solve the cube layer by layer, we start by making a cross. We'll use white for the colour of the cross. As you get better at solving the cube, you can try other colours for the cross too.

This is what it will look like when all the white cross pieces are in their correct spot.

In this tutorial you have two choices for how to learn the cross. You can choose to follow a step-by-step process or learn how to create your own process.

Option 1: Follow a step-by-step process.

The way we'll be solving the cross is called the Daisy Method. We make a shape that looks like a daisy flower. This helps us put all the cross pieces in the right place more easily.

When we look at the cube, every cross piece is either a "good edge" or a "bad edge".

Once we've made the daisy, it's time to put each cross piece into their correct spots.

Pick one of the cross pieces and line up the other colour with the center in the equator. Then we can turn it twice to bring it into its correct spot.

Example: Solving the white-green cross piece.

We do the same thing for all the cross pieces in any order we want. Then we're done the cross!

Example: 1. white-green, 2. white-red, 3. white-orange, 4. white-blue

Option 2: Create your own process.

The step-by-step process (Option 1) involves making a daisy shape before putting each cross piece in its correct spot.

Instead of doing this, we can put each cross in its correct spot right away. This is trickier, but it's faster once you get used to it.

When we look at the cube, every cross piece is either a "good edge" or a "bad edge". "Good" means we can put the white sticker on the white face with just one turn.

Here's an example of a good edge.

This is also a good edge, because turning a face twice counts as one turn.

We start by bringing one good edge to the white face.

Now there two choices for what to do next:

  1. Put the cross piece in its correct spot. (Easier, but slower.)
  2. Put another cross piece (ideally a good edge) on the white face. (Harder, but faster.)

If you choose to put the cross piece in its correct spot, you should put all other cross pieces in their correct spots right away.

If you choose to put another cross piece on the white face, make sure it goes in the correct spot **relative** to the first cross piece. We can look at the centers to tell us the order of these colours.

When we run out of good edges, we can work on the bad edges.

The fastest way to solve the cross involves figuring out the best order to do the cross pieces. It also involves working on more than one cross piece at a time. The cross can always be solved in 8 turns or less. The more you practice, the better you will get at solving the cross quickly.

Before moving on to the next step, it's important that you are confident solving the cross. After you have practiced it for a while, you can either watch the bonus video to learn a faster way to solve the cross, or you can move on to the next step: solving the rest of the first layer.
