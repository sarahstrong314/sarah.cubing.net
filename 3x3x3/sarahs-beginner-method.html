Sarah's Beginner Method

This is a tutorial on how to solve a Rubik's Cube for those who have little or no experience with cubing.

Unlike most Rubik's Cube tutorials, this one is driven by current research in the field of education. The author is both an educator and a speedcuber.

This webpage is currently in progress. It will be fully completed in January 2025.

It's a good idea to learn how to write down the turns we make so that we can communicate more easily. We're going to learn something called "standard notation". "Notation" means we're using letters, numbers, and symbols. "Standard" means the most common way.

When we turn a face, we write the first letter of the face. If we're turning it clockwise, we don't need to write anything else.

To know whether we're turning a face clockwise or counter-clockwise, rotate the cube to look at the center of the face to see which way we're turning it.

R (right face clockwise) L (left face clockwise) U (upper face clockwise)

When we turn a face the other way, we write a symbol that looks like an apostrophe. We say "prime" for the symbol.

R' (right face counterclockwise) L' (left face counterclockwise) U' (upper face counterclockwise)

An algorithm is a list of turns put together. The two algorithms we just saw are L' U' L and R U R'.

R U R' (right flick) L' U' L (left flick)

These are the only two algorithms we need to solve all the first layer corners. We'll give them nicknames since we'll be using them a lot. R U R' is right flick and L' U' L is left flick.

When we use these flicks, we need to hold the cube the correct way. For the right flick, make sure the corner is on the right. For the left flick, make sure it's on the left.

The cube in Twisty Player will be slightly rotated to the left for lefty algorithms and slightly rotated to the right for righty algorithms.

A lefty algorithm. In this example, green is the front face and orange is the left face. A righty algorithm. In this example, orange is the front face and green is the right face.

Next: 5 – Flipping the Yellow Edges

Flipping the Yellow Edges

After we've solved the first two layers, we can make all the yellow edge stickers face up.

This is what it will look like when the yellow edges are all flipped correctly. We will be making a yellow cross.

So far we have solved F2L. This includes the white cross and four F2L pairs. Each pair is a corner on the first layer attached to the edge above it in the equator.

We will be solving the entire last layer just by taking out the pairs and putting them back in differently.

Each pair will stay together from now on. If you want, you tape together the two pieces of each pair. This can help you figure out if you made a mistake partway through an algorithm.

We're going to be solving the last layer using only three new algorithms.

Each of these algorithms:

  • keep the four F2L pairs together
  • are seven turns or less
  • can be transformed to create new algorithms
  • are used by speedcubers

We have seen R, L, U in algorithms. We're now going to see F too. The letter F stands for the front face.

F (front face clockwise) F' (front face counterclockwise)

We're going to learn about something called an inverse. Whenever we undo a turn we just did, we call that the inverse.

For example, we can undo F with F'.

F F'

We can also undo an algorithm.

If we do F R, we can undo that with R' F'.

F R R' F'

Notice that we do the turns in reverse order and we do each of them in the opposite direction.

If we do F R U, we can undo that with U' R' F'.

F R U U' R' F'

The algorithm F R U U' R' F' happens to look very similar to the shortest algorithm that moves around pieces in the last layer. All we need to do is swap the U' and R'.

F R U R' U' F'

When we do F R U R' U' F', we flip some of the yellow edges and move around most of the yellow pieces. Notice how the F2L pairs stay together throughout the algorithm.

One of the ways we can transform an algorithm is by mirroring it. For us, this will mean taking a right-handed algorithm and making it left-handed. Or taking a left-handed algorithm and making it right-handed.

The mirror of F R U R' U' F' is F' L' U' L U F.

F R U R' U' F'
F' L' U' L U F

F R U R' U' F' and F' L' U' L U F are useful because they change which way the yellow edges are flipped. In this step, we will be flipping the yellow edges so that they are all facing up to make a yellow cross.

We use these algorithms when we have two yellow edges facing up and they form a line. Pick F R U R' U' F' if you are right-handed and pick F' L' U' L U F if you are left-handed. Hold the cube so that the line is horizontal, then do either algorithm.

F R U R' U' F'
F' L' U' L U F

We learned about inverses earlier. Inverting is another way to transform an algorithm, just like mirroring.

If we have two yellow edges and they form an "L" shape instead of a line, we can use the inverse of F R U R' U' F' or F' L' U' L U F. The inverses are F U R U' R' F' and F' U' L' U L F.

If you are left-handed, hold the cube so that you see an L. If you're right-handed, hold the cube so that you see a backward L.

F U R U' R' F'
F' U' L' U L F
Sometimes we don't get a line or an L. If we get a dot, we can do F R U R' U' F' of any of its transformations. Then, we will get a line or an L and we can solve it like before.

It is impossible to solve the cube when there are exactly one or three yellow stickers facing up. If you have this, it is likely because the pieces were taken apart and put back in wrong. It can be fixed by taking the pieces back out again and putting them back in incorrectly.
